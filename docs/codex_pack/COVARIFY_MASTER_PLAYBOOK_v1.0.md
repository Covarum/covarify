# 00 - Start Here

**Status:** LOCKED

Last reviewed: July 17, 2026

## What this playbook must do

The playbook must make it possible for a founder, engineer, designer,
advisor, partner, or future employee to answer five questions without
reconstructing old conversations:

> **1.** What is Covarify?
>
> **2.** Who is it for and what problem does it solve?
>
> **3.** How should the product think and behave?
>
> **4.** How does the business make money without compromising trust?
>
> **5.** What is being built now, later, or not at all?

## What this playbook is not

It is not a collection of polished language that becomes disconnected
from the product. It is not a generic startup template. It is not a
substitute for legal counsel, a security program, or feature-level
requirements.

## The playbook constitution

These principles should survive individual features and interface
changes.

### 1. Clarity over complexity

The user should leave knowing what matters, why it matters, and what
they can do next. More data is not automatically more clarity.

### 2. Calm over clutter

The interface should lower cognitive load. Urgency should be used only
when the underlying situation is urgent.

### 3. Trust over hype

Covarify should state what it knows, what it inferred, what it does not
know, and how confident it is. It should never imply certainty that the
data cannot support.

### 4. Meaning before information

People experience money through rent, children, work, health,
relationships, debt, goals, repairs, and life changes. Transactions are
evidence. Financial Events provide meaning.

### 5. Strength before deficit

Covarify should not greet people by telling them what is wrong. It
should begin with a real strength, useful context, or completed step
before surfacing pressure.

### 6. Every insight should lead somewhere useful

A discovery without a practical next move becomes another notification.
The next move may be an action, a comparison, a question, an
organization step, or a decision to wait.

### 7. Consequences must be visible

Paying a minimum instead of a statement balance, consolidating debt,
pausing savings, using a hardship plan, or borrowing against an asset
can create relief and new costs at the same time. Covarify must show
both.

### 8. The user stays in control

No automatic money movement, account closure, debt application, or
high-consequence action should occur without explicit consent and a
clear preview. The user can correct, decline, export, disconnect, and
delete.

### 9. Empowerment, not dependence

The ecosystem should become relied upon because it is useful and
remembers context, not because it creates anxiety or hides information.
Success means users become better at understanding their own decisions.

### 10. The narrowest honest claim wins

Covarify should not call itself a bank, financial advisor, credit repair
company, debt settlement company, lender, or investment manager unless
and until the legal and operating structure supports that claim.

### 11. Conversation is the interface

Users should be able to explain money the way it exists in their heads.
Covarify should convert speech or text into structured, reviewable
financial context, ask only the minimum clarifying question, update
downstream analysis, and show what changed. Conversation must create
useful state - not merely produce a plausible answer.

## Source-of-truth ritual

At the end of every meaningful sprint:

> **•** Update the current-state snapshot.
>
> **•** Record product decisions and rejected alternatives.
>
> **•** Mark assumptions that became evidence.
>
> **•** Mark evidence that invalidated assumptions.
>
> **•** Retire outdated wording rather than leaving contradictions.
>
> **•** Add new recommendation risks to the action library.

## Decision quality standard

A material decision is complete only when it records:

> **•** Context and user problem.
>
> **•** Decision.
>
> **•** Alternatives considered.
>
> **•** Why this choice was made.
>
> **•** Risks and tradeoffs.
>
> **•** What evidence would cause the decision to change.
>
> **•** Owner and review date.

## Mission

Help people make better financial decisions when money and life are too
interconnected for a simple budget to explain.

## Vision

Every household should have a calm, trusted financial operating system
that understands the whole picture, remembers what matters, and helps
the next decision feel possible.

## Strategic thesis

Most consumer finance products are organized around accounts,
categories, dashboards, scores, or individual goals. Real financial
pressure is organized around competing obligations and timing:

> **•** Rent is due before income lands.
>
> **•** A person wants to pay down debt but cannot afford to recreate a
> cash shortage.
>
> **•** A credit card statement balance, minimum payment, and
> consolidation offer each solve a different problem.
>
> **•** A family expense may be financially flexible but emotionally
> non-negotiable.
>
> **•** A "bad" month may reflect a one-time life event rather than a
> behavior problem.

Covarify wins by interpreting the relationships between cash flow, debt,
timing, obligations, goals, and life context - then helping the user
compare realistic actions and consequences.

## Defining differentiator - speak directly to the financial picture

Most financial products require the user to translate real life into the
product's structure: find the transaction, choose a category, open a
form, select a rule, and remember to update the forecast. Covarify
reverses that burden. The user can speak or type naturally, and Covarify
does the financial organization behind the conversation.

A user can say:

| **"I spent \$100 at Dick's Sporting Goods for softball stuff for my kid."** |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|

Covarify should identify or create the relevant transaction, understand
the amount, merchant, person, and purpose, attach it to the appropriate
Financial Event, distinguish it from ordinary shopping, update the
financial picture, and remember the context. If recurrence or the
matching transaction is ambiguous, it asks one short question. It then
confirms the change and makes it easy to undo.

The defining combination is:

| **Natural conversation + connected financial data + life context + financial memory + scenario modeling + explainable next moves.** |
|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

Generic AI can answer generic money questions. Covarify understands the
user's actual financial state and turns the conversation into a safer,
more useful financial decision.

## The wedge

The first wedge is not "everyone who has money." It is:

| **People who are capable of making decisions but cannot see the full financial picture clearly enough to know which decision should come first.** |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

The earliest high-value use case is **cash-flow and debt pressure with
competing priorities**. This is where a static dashboard is least
helpful and a decision system can create immediate value.

## Durable differentiation

The moat is not bank connection or transaction categorization. Those can
be reproduced. The durable system is the combination of:

> **1. Conversational financial interface** - users can speak or type
> naturally, and the interaction becomes structured, reviewable
> financial context rather than disposable chat.
>
> **2. Financial context graph** - how accounts, transactions,
> liabilities, income, documents, people, goals, and events relate.
>
> **3. Decision history** - what options were considered, what the user
> chose, and what happened.
>
> **4. User-declared constraints** - expenses or commitments the user
> will not cut, timing realities, household priorities, and risk
> tolerance.
>
> **5. Recommendation policy** - transparent logic for when to
> stabilize, repair, optimize, or build.
>
> **6. Trust history** - consistent explanations, visible confidence,
> user corrections, and no hidden incentives.
>
> **7. Financial memory** - the product gets more useful because it
> remembers meaning, not merely because it stores more rows.

## Strategic principles

### Build the decision layer before the breadth layer

A thin but excellent decision experience is more valuable than ten
shallow dashboards. The first product should make one difficult decision
materially clearer.

### Use connection as a means, not the product

Plaid is an input. It is not the value proposition. Manual input remains
a legitimate path when connection is unavailable, unwanted, or
incomplete.

### Do not confuse engagement with usefulness

The goal is not daily screen time. The goal is timely return at a moment
of decision, plus enough ongoing awareness to prevent surprises.

### Earn expansion

Covarify can later expand into household collaboration, financial
documents, employer financial wellness, and professional handoffs.
Expansion should follow demonstrated trust and retention in the initial
decision use case.

# 02 - Customer and Market

## Primary design customer

The initial design customer is a financially overloaded adult, often
managing a household, who has enough information to feel pressure but
not enough integrated clarity to make confident decisions.

Typical characteristics:

> **•** Multiple accounts, debts, subscriptions, and due dates.
>
> **•** Fixed obligations that leave little room for error.
>
> **•** Income that may vary, arrive unevenly, or come from multiple
> sources.
>
> **•** Family, health, work, housing, or relationship obligations that
> do not fit a clean budget category.
>
> **•** A pattern of making one "responsible" decision that creates a
> new short-term problem elsewhere.
>
> **•** Shame or fatigue from tools that reduce everything to
> overspending.
>
> **•** A desire for support without surrendering control.

## Core jobs to be done

### Functional jobs

> **•** Show me the complete picture without making me assemble it
> manually.
>
> **•** Let me explain a transaction, correction, or decision in my own
> words instead of making me translate it into a form.
>
> **•** Tell me what is creating pressure now.
>
> **•** Help me choose what to do first.
>
> **•** Let me compare realistic choices before I act.
>
> **•** Warn me when a choice solves this month by worsening the next
> one.
>
> **•** Remember what I decided and revisit it at the right time.
>
> **•** Keep the documents and context attached to the financial event.

### Emotional jobs

> **•** Help me feel less ashamed and less alone.
>
> **•** Make the problem feel finite and understandable.
>
> **•** Give me proof that progress is possible.
>
> **•** Respect the parts of my life that cannot be optimized away.
>
> **•** Help me regain agency without pretending every option is easy.

### Social jobs

> **•** Help me explain a decision to a partner, parent, child,
> creditor, or professional.
>
> **•** Give me a shared picture without forcing me to disclose
> everything at once.
>
> **•** Help me ask better questions when I need human advice.

## Initial use-case segments

### Segment A - Cash-flow pressure plus debt

The user is paying bills, carrying revolving debt, and trying to decide
whether extra debt payments are helping or creating another shortage.

**Primary moment:** "Should I pay this card in full, pay the minimum, or
use the money somewhere else first?"

### Segment B - Variable or fragmented income

The user is self-employed, commission-based, hourly, seasonal, or
combining multiple income sources.

**Primary moment:** "I make enough over time, so why am I always behind
at the wrong time?"

### Segment C - Household complexity

The user manages children, aging parents, shared finances, support
payments, medical costs, or a partner with different financial behavior.

**Primary moment:** "The numbers look possible on paper, but real life
keeps changing the order."

### Segment D - Major life event

The user is facing a move, job change, separation, medical event,
vehicle purchase, business launch, college cost, or home repair.

**Primary moment:** "What can I safely do without breaking everything
else?"

### Segment E - Positive margin without progress

The user technically has positive cash flow but does not see consistent
movement toward reserves or debt reduction.

**Primary moment:** "Where is the margin going, and which goal should
receive it?"

## Secondary design lens - neurodivergent-friendly

Covarify should be exceptionally usable for people with ADHD, autism,
executive-function challenges, anxiety, or financial avoidance. This is
a design advantage, not a reason to limit the market.

Design implications:

> **•** One decision at a time.
>
> **•** Visible completion and progress.
>
> **•** Short explanations with optional depth.
>
> **•** Reduced sensory clutter.
>
> **•** No ambiguous urgency.
>
> **•** Persistent context so the user does not have to restart.
>
> **•** Concrete next steps and review dates.
>
> **•** Flexible reminders without punishment language.

## Who is not the initial customer

> **•** Active traders seeking portfolio analytics.
>
> **•** Businesses seeking accounting, payroll, or treasury systems.
>
> **•** Users primarily seeking automated bill pay or money movement.
>
> **•** Users seeking a credit repair dispute engine.
>
> **•** Users seeking a tax return preparation product.
>
> **•** People who only want a spending tracker and do not value
> decision support.

Covarify should not position itself as a better version of a generic

## Signature customer behavior

The customer should not need to think like a database. A normal
interaction is:

| **"I spent \$100 at Dick's Sporting Goods for softball stuff for my kid."** |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|

Covarify handles the translation from real life into financial
structure. It finds or creates the transaction, records who and what it

confirms the result. The cash outflow does not disappear, but the
purchase is no longer misread as generic shopping or an unexplained

The customer promise is not merely "ask a finance question." It is:

| **Explain your financial life naturally. Covarify organizes the meaning and does the financial work behind the answer.** |

# 03 - Product System

**Status:** WORKING with core objects LOCKED

## The Covarify clarity loop

Covarify should operate as a repeating loop, not a one-time report.

> **1. Connect or enter** - Bring in the minimum financial information
> needed.
>
> **2. Speak or type context** - Let the user explain what happened,
> what something means, or what they need to decide.
>
> **3. Understand** - Normalize the money picture and convert confirmed
> meaning into structured context.
>
> **4. Discover** - Identify pressure, opportunity, inconsistency, or
> missing information.
>
> **5. Decide** - Compare realistic options and consequences through
> conversation and controls.
>
> **6. Act** - Choose a manageable next move.
>
> **7. Remember** - Preserve the decision, rationale, constraints,
> context, and review date.
>
> **8. Reassess** - Measure what changed and determine the next move.

## Product ecosystem

### 1. Conversational Financial Interface

The primary meaning-capture and decision interface. Users can speak or
type in ordinary language to:

> **•** Explain what a transaction was really for.
>
> **•** Add a missing cash or manual transaction.
>
> **•** Correct a category, person, purpose, event, or recurrence
> assumption.
>
> **•** State a constraint such as "I cannot cut this."
>
> **•** Ask a question grounded in their actual financial picture.
>
> **•** Explore a scenario such as "What if I only pay the minimum this
> month?"

The interface is not a chat window that only returns text. It resolves
the user's intent against financial evidence, proposes or applies an
authorized structured update, recomputes downstream analysis, and
confirms what changed with an undo path.

### 2. Money Picture

The normalized view of accounts, available cash, liabilities, income,
outflows, recurring obligations, and timing.

The Money Picture answers:

> **•** What is connected?
>
> **•** What is liquid now?
>
> **•** What is owed?
>
> **•** What money is arriving and when?
>
> **•** What obligations are due and when?
>
> **•** How complete is the picture?

### 3. Financial Brain

The domain layer that interprets relationships across financial and life
context. It should know that a transaction may be evidence of a broader
Financial Event, not the entire meaning of that event.

### 4. Discovery Engine

The system that identifies observations worth the user's attention. A
discovery must include evidence, confidence, and why it matters.

Examples:

> **•** Outflows exceeded inflows in the analyzed period.
>
> **•** The deficit is concentrated in timing rather than total monthly
> income.
>
> **•** A large purchase appears to be a one-time event, not a recurring
> trend.
>
> **•** An extra debt payment reduced the buffer below upcoming
> essential bills.
>
> **•** A recurring expense has not been used or confirmed.
>
> **•** A goal is being funded while required minimums are at risk.

### 5. Decision Engine

The structured system that compares actions, estimates consequences,
applies guardrails, and produces a clear recommendation plus
alternatives.

### 6. First Win

The first evidence-backed action or decision that makes the user's
situation meaningfully clearer or safer. It should be achievable,
explainable, and revisited.

### 7. Next Best Move

The action recommended after the First Win or at a later decision point.
It should reflect the latest data, user constraints, and prior outcomes.

### 8. Financial Life Vault

The organized memory layer for documents, receipts, policies, invoices,
warranties, notes, and records attached to Financial Events, accounts,
people, or decisions.

### 9. Financial Zoom

A progressive-detail system that lets the user move from a calm summary
into evidence, transaction detail, assumptions, and scenario math
without forcing all detail onto the first screen.

## Financial Event is the primary meaning object

Transactions answer what happened. Financial Events answer what it
meant.

Example:

| **Transaction view**     | **Financial Event view**                                         |
|--------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Home Depot - \$257.69    | Backyard Refresh                                                 |
| Merchant, date, category | Project, receipts, photos, notes, purpose, people, tax relevance |
| One row                  | A retrievable context bundle                                     |

## Signature conversation-to-context behavior

**User says:**

| **"I spent \$100 at Dick's Sporting Goods for softball stuff for my kid."** |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|

**Covarify handles:**

> **1.** Match the most likely connected Dick's transaction by merchant,
> amount, date, and account, or create a manual transaction when no
> imported record exists.
>
> **2.** Extract the explicit facts: \$100, Dick's Sporting Goods,
> child, softball, and equipment or supplies.
>
> **3.** Replace a generic category such as Shopping with a more useful
> context such as Child - Softball Equipment.
>
> **4.** Attach the transaction to a Softball Season Financial Event and
> the relevant household person when the user has provided or confirmed
> that relationship.
>
> **5.** Preserve the financial fact that \$100 left the account while
> preventing the item from being treated as unexplained everyday
> shopping.
>
> **6.** Ask one targeted question only when a material detail is
> ambiguous, such as whether the expense is one-time or likely to repeat
> during the season.
>
> **7.** Recompute cash flow, spending patterns, forecasts, discoveries,
> and recommendations affected by the change.
>
> **8.** Confirm the update in plain language and provide Change or
> Undo.

**Example confirmation:**

| **"Got it. I matched the \$100 Dick's purchase and labeled it as your child's softball equipment under Softball Season. The \$100 still counts against available cash, but I will not treat it as ordinary shopping. I marked recurrence as 'needs confirmation.' Change \| Undo"** |
|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

If multiple transactions match, Covarify should show the candidates
rather than guessing. If a manually entered transaction later arrives
through Plaid, the system should reconcile and deduplicate it.

This prevents Covarify from reducing every life moment to a cost. Money
can lead when it is central to the decision and remain secondary when
the event is primarily about context or memory.

## Required product behavior

### Conversation must create reviewable structured state

A conversational interaction is complete only when Covarify has done one
of three things: answered from verified data without changing state,
proposed or applied a visible structured update, or asked the minimum
clarifying question required to proceed safely. The product should never
imply that it "handled" a correction when only chat text changed.

### Data completeness comes before confidence

Every analysis should know which relevant accounts and periods are
missing. A confident recommendation from an incomplete picture is a
trust failure.

### User correction becomes structured data

When a user changes a category, marks an expense as essential, says an
event will not recur, or declines an action, the product should remember
the reason where appropriate.

### Recommendations are versioned

A recommendation should preserve the data window, assumptions, engine
version, and user choices that produced it. Later recommendations can
then explain what changed.

### No silent meaning inference

AI may extract purpose, relationships, and context the user stated
explicitly. It may suggest additional meaning or recurrence, but it
should not silently invent it. High-confidence low-risk updates may be
applied with immediate confirmation and Undo; ambiguous or
decision-changing assumptions require a short clarifying question or
explicit confirmation.

## Product boundaries for MVP

### In scope

> **•** Plaid-based and manual money picture.
>

# 04 - Decision Engine and First Win Playbook

**Status:** WORKING - Highest-priority product definition

## Product correction

The First Win cannot stop at "find three categories to cut." That is
useful for some users, but it is not robust enough for the financial
decisions Covarify is meant to support.

The product must be able to recognize that getting back on track may
involve a combination of:

> **•** Reducing or not repeating selected flexible transactions.
>
> **•** Pausing an optional extra debt payment.
>
> **•** Paying a required minimum instead of the full statement balance
> for a limited period.
>
> **•** Moving a due date or requesting a temporary hardship option.
>
> **•** Adjusting the timing of a savings transfer.
>
> **•** Accelerating or stabilizing income.
>
> **•** Comparing consolidation, refinance, balance-transfer, or
> debt-management options.
>
> **•** Using a one-time resource only after showing its consequences.

The user should be able to test these choices before committing.
Covarify's job is not to celebrate a mathematically closed gap if the
choice creates a larger problem next month.

## First Win definition

| **A First Win is the smallest evidence-backed decision or action that materially improves stability, clarity, or momentum without hiding a foreseeable consequence.** |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

A First Win should be:

> **•** Relevant to the user's immediate decision.
>
> **•** Achievable within seven days or clearly staged.
>
> **•** Supported by visible evidence.
>
> **•** Honest about assumptions and uncertainty.
>
> **•** Reversible when possible.
>
> **•** Safe relative to the user's obligations.
>
> **•** Measurable at the review date.

## Decision ladder

Covarify should classify the user's current need before choosing an
intervention.

### Level 1 - Stabilize

Protect essentials, required minimums, immediate liquidity, and critical
coverage. Avoid overdrafts, missed housing or utility payments, lapse of
necessary insurance, or compounding emergency pressure.

### Level 2 - Repair

Close a recurring cash-flow gap through a combination of spending
choices, timing changes, minimum-payment strategy, bill adjustments, and
income actions.

### Level 3 - Optimize

Reduce interest, simplify debt, improve payment sequencing, increase
reserves, or restructure obligations after stability exists.

### Level 4 - Build

Direct a durable positive margin toward reserves, goals, debt reduction,
protection, or long-term wealth.

**Rule:** Covarify should not jump to Optimize or Build while a known
Stabilize problem remains unresolved unless the user knowingly chooses
the tradeoff.

## Analysis sequence

> **1.** Confirm data window and completeness.
>
> **2.** Identify available cash and near-term timing.
>
> **3.** Separate inflows, essential outflows, required payments,
> flexible outflows, transfers, and unknowns.
>
> **4.** Detect one-time events and duplicate or pending items.
>
> **5.** Identify the decision horizon - next seven days, next pay
> cycle, trailing 30 days, or longer.
>
> **6.** Calculate the gap or margin.
>
> **7.** Ask the user to confirm constraints and misclassifications.
>
> **8.** Generate realistic action candidates.
>
> **9.** Model combinations, not only individual cuts.
>
> **10.** Rank scenarios by safety, impact, reversibility, burden, and
> user preference.
>
> **11.** Present a recommendation plus alternatives.
>
> **12.** Record the decision and schedule reassessment.

## Conversation-to-decision loop

Users should be able to change the decision model by speaking or typing
naturally:

> **•** "That Dick's purchase was softball equipment for my kid, not
> normal shopping."
>
> **•** "I cannot cut the kids' activities. Show me another option."
>
> **•** "That deposit was reimbursement, not income."
>
> **•** "What happens if I pay only the minimum this month?"
>
> **•** "I need \$2,000 by the end of August. Is that realistic?"

For each turn, Covarify should identify whether the user is asking,
correcting, adding context, setting a constraint, or requesting a
scenario. The conversational layer may interpret language, but
deterministic services should perform the financial calculation and
state change. Covarify then restates the interpreted change, shows the
effect on the recommendation, and preserves the user's correction in
decision memory.

A conversation that changes a recommendation must be versioned with the
original evidence, interpreted context, resulting state change, and user
confirmation or undo.

## Interactive scenario board

The First Win screen should evolve into a user-controlled planning
surface.

### Transaction controls

Each relevant flexible transaction or recurring item can include:

> **• Not repeatable** - One-time event; exclude from forward estimate.
>
> **• Can stop** - Remove future recurrence.
>
> **• Can reduce** - Choose an amount or percentage.
>
> **• Must keep** - User-defined constraint.
>
> **• Unsure** - Preserve for review rather than assuming.

### Payment controls

For each liability or bill where data is available:

> **•** Required minimum.
>
> **•** Statement balance or scheduled payment.
>
> **•** Optional extra amount.
>
> **•** Due date.
>
> **•** Autopay status.
>
> **•** User-selected payment amount.
>
> **•** Estimated interest or balance consequence when available.
>
> **•** Risk of missed-payment or late-fee impact.

### Timing controls

> **•** Move payment date - only as a planning scenario until creditor
> confirmation.
>
> **•** Match payment to income date.
>
> **•** Delay a discretionary transfer.
>
> **•** Split a payment across pay cycles when permitted.
>
> **•** Mark expected irregular income.

### Progress meter

The board should show:

> **•** Current gap or margin.
>
> **•** Amount improved by selected actions.
>
> **•** Remaining gap.
>
> **•** Effect on essential-payment coverage.
>
> **•** Effect on required minimums.
>
> **•** Estimated next-cycle consequence.
>
> **•** Confidence and missing information.

### Required outcome

The user should leave with:

> **•** A chosen scenario.
>
> **•** A plain-language reason.
>
> **•** A short action checklist.
>
> **•** What not to do.
>
> **•** A review date.
>
> **•** An alternative if the primary plan fails.

## Action risk levels

### Low consequence

Usually reversible and unlikely to create material downstream harm.

Examples:

> **•** Cancel or pause a nonessential subscription.
>
> **•** Reduce a flexible category.
>
> **•** Mark a one-time purchase as non-recurring.
>
> **•** Move an internal goal date.
>
> **•** Review a bill or request a due-date change.

**Product behavior:** Covarify may recommend directly with a clear
estimate.

### Moderate consequence

May create cost, credit utilization, reduced flexibility, or service
changes.

Examples:

> **•** Pay a required minimum instead of the full statement balance for
> one cycle.
>
> **•** Pause an optional extra debt payment.
>
> **•** Reduce a savings contribution.
>
> **•** Use a balance transfer.
>
> **•** Enter a creditor hardship arrangement.
>
> **•** Consolidate or refinance unsecured debt.
>
> **•** Enroll in a debt management plan.

**Product behavior:** Show at least one alternative, the cost or
uncertainty, duration, reversal path, and conditions that make it
appropriate or inappropriate.

### High consequence

Can create tax, legal, collateral, credit, coverage, or long-term
financial harm.

Examples:

> **•** Debt settlement.
>
> **•** Cash advance or high-cost short-term borrowing.
>
> **•** Retirement-plan withdrawal or loan.
>
> **•** Borrowing against life insurance or another asset.
>
> **•** Secured debt used to pay unsecured debt.
>
> **•** Surrendering insurance coverage or a financial contract.
>
> **•** Bankruptcy-related decisions.

**Product behavior:** Education and scenario comparison only unless a
qualified, appropriately structured human professional is involved.
Require explicit warnings and do not frame one option as effortless
relief.

## Core decision pathways

### Pathway A - Negative cash flow

> **1.** Confirm whether the gap is recurring, timing-based, or
> one-time.
>
> **2.** Protect housing, utilities, food, transportation needed for
> income, medical needs, critical insurance, and required minimums.
>
> **3.** Remove pending duplicates, transfers, and one-time items from
> forward projection.
>
> **4.** Ask the user to confirm flexible candidates.
>
> **5.** Compare spending changes with payment-timing and income
> actions.
>
> **6.** If flexible spending cannot close the gap, say so clearly.
>
> **7.** Present a fallback path rather than implying the user failed to
> cut enough.

### Pathway B - Full balance versus minimum payment

Covarify should not use a blanket rule.

Evaluate:

> **•** Required minimum and due date.
>
> **•** Cash needed for essential obligations before the next income
> event.
>
> **•** Existing buffer.
>
> **•** Interest cost and grace-period implications when known.
>
> **•** Whether the payment is optional extra, statement balance, or
> required amount.
>
> **•** Risk of new charges recreating the balance.
>
> **•** Whether the user has a defined date to resume higher payments.

A possible First Win may be:

| **Preserve enough cash to cover essential obligations and required minimums through the next income date, pay the card's required minimum or a custom safe amount this cycle, avoid new charges, and reassess immediately after the next deposit.** |
|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

This is not "minimum payments forever." It is a time-limited
stabilization decision with a visible cost and review date.

### Pathway C - Consolidation or refinance

Before presenting consolidation as better, compare:

> **•** New rate versus effective existing rates.
>
> **•** Origination, transfer, and annual fees.
>
> **•** New term and total projected cost.
>
> **•** Monthly payment and cash-flow relief.
>
> **•** Whether cards remain open and available for reuse.
>
> **•** Qualification uncertainty and credit inquiry.
>
> **•** Secured versus unsecured status.
>
> **•** Loss of creditor hardship protections or promotional terms.
>
> **•** Behavior plan that prevents re-accumulation.

Covarify should distinguish:

> **•** Lower payment because the rate improved.
>
> **•** Lower payment because the term became longer.
>
> **•** Lower payment because debt became secured.
>
> **•** Temporary promotional relief.

### Pathway D - Debt management or hardship

Present when minimums are not sustainable or interest makes progress
unlikely. Explain that creditor participation, account status, access to
credit, and fees may vary. The product should help prepare questions and
compare verified terms, not imply guaranteed acceptance.

### Pathway E - Positive cash-flow margin

> **1.** Confirm the margin is durable and not caused by missing
> obligations.
>
> **2.** Protect a near-term buffer.
>
> **3.** Identify the highest-pressure debt or goal.
>
> **4.** Offer a deliberate allocation rather than allowing the margin
> to disappear.
>
> **5.** Schedule the transfer or action only with user consent.

### Pathway F - Income timing problem

> **1.** Map expected income dates and confidence.
>
> **2.** Map essential due dates.
>
> **3.** Identify due-date changes, split payments, billing-cycle
> choices, or temporary buffer needs.
>
> **4.** Avoid describing a timing mismatch as overspending if monthly
> income is otherwise sufficient.

### Pathway G - One-time financial event

> **1.** Link the transactions to the event.
>
> **2.** Separate event cost from recurring baseline.
>
> **3.** Preserve receipts and context.
>
> **4.** Decide whether the event creates a temporary recovery plan or a
> permanent budget change.

## Recommendation contract

Every meaningful recommendation should contain:

| **Field**            | **Requirement**                                                             |
|----------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Headline             | One clear decision, not a vague insight                                     |
| Diagnosis            | What the evidence shows                                                     |
| Evidence             | Accounts, dates, categories, and amounts used                               |
| Assumptions          | What Covarify had to infer or estimate                                      |
| User context         | Relevant spoken or typed corrections, constraints, and confirmed meaning    |
| Primary action       | The recommended next move                                                   |
| Alternatives         | At least one realistic alternative for moderate or high-consequence choices |
| Estimated impact     | Range or estimate, not false precision                                      |
| Tradeoffs            | Cost, delay, risk, or lost opportunity                                      |
| Safeguards           | What must remain protected                                                  |
| Confidence           | High, medium, or low with reason                                            |
| Seven-day plan       | Concrete steps                                                              |
| Review date          | When the recommendation should be reassessed                                |
| Disclaimer / handoff | Required when professional review is appropriate                            |

## Recommendation ranking model

Candidate scenarios should be scored across:

> **•** Safety of essential obligations.
>
> **•** Required-payment protection.
>
> **•** Size of improvement.
>
> **•** Durability.
>
> **•** Reversibility.
>
> **•** User burden.
>
> **•** User-stated preference.
>
> **•** Cost and downstream risk.
>
> **•** Data confidence.
>
> **•** Time to impact.

A scenario with the largest immediate dollar improvement should not
automatically win.

## Empowerment loop

>
> **•** Mark the action as planned, completed, changed, or declined.
>
> **•** Reassess at the promised date.
>
> **•** Compare estimated versus actual outcome.
>
> **•** Explain what Covarify learned.

The product should become more personal through consented learning, not
hidden profiling.

# 05 - User Experience, Conversation, and Brand Voice

**Status:** WORKING with core principles LOCKED

## Experience goal

Covarify should feel like a smart, calm person helping the user see the
whole board - not like a bank dashboard, accounting package, debt alarm,
or judgmental budget coach.

The defining experience is that the user can speak or type the way they
naturally think. They should not need to know the right category, field,
financial term, or screen before explaining what happened.

## Onboarding philosophy

### Start with the decision, not the data request

Ask why the user is here before asking for every account. The first
screen should make the experience feel relevant.

Recommended opening:

> **•** What decision feels hardest right now?
>
> **•** What would clarity help you do?
>
> **•** How involved do you want Covarify to be?

Then explain why connection or manual entry helps answer that question.

### Connection first, but not connection only

Plaid can be the primary path because it reduces work and improves
evidence. Manual input should remain available and respected. The
product should not punish users whose institutions are unavailable or
who prefer limited disclosure.

### Progressive consent

Do not ask for broad access without explaining:

> **•** What data is requested.
>
> **•** Why it is needed.
>
> **•** How it will be used.
>
> **•** What is optional.
>
> **•** How to disconnect or delete.

### Deliver value quickly

The user should see a meaningful first picture within about ten minutes
when data is ready. Incomplete data should produce an honest setup
state, not a fabricated recommendation.

## Interaction model

### One primary decision per screen

The interface may contain supporting context, but one action should
clearly lead.

### Summary first, evidence on demand

Use Financial Zoom:

> **1.** Calm summary.
>
> **2.** Why Covarify sees it.
>
> **3.** Transactions and assumptions.
>
> **4.** Scenario math.
>
> **5.** Technical or policy detail.

### Interactive, not prescriptive

The user should be able to say:

> **•** "I cannot cut this."
>
> **•** "That will not happen again."
>
> **•** "This payment is optional extra."
>
> **•** "That income is not reliable."
>
> **•** "Show me another way."

The product should update immediately and explain what changed.

### No shame mechanics

Avoid:

> **•** Red warning language for ordinary uncertainty.
>
> **•** "Bad spending" labels.
>
> **•** Confetti for cutting something emotionally meaningful.
>
> **•** Streak loss or punishment for missed actions.
>
> **•** False urgency designed to increase engagement.
>
> **•** Messages that imply discipline is the only reason a plan failed.

Use:

> **•** "Here is what changed."
>
> **•** "This option creates more room now but costs more over time."
>
> **•** "Flexible spending alone does not appear sufficient."
>
> **•** "You marked this as important, so Covarify kept it protected."

## Conversational product interaction

### Core interface

Every meaningful financial surface should provide a visible **Tell
Covarify** or **Ask Covarify** entry point with text and microphone
controls. The same interface can be opened globally or from a specific
transaction, account, decision, or Financial Event.

The product must distinguish three intents:

> **1. Ask** - Answer a question from the current verified picture
> without changing data.
>
> **2. Tell or correct** - Add context or propose a structured state
> change.
>
> **3. Explore** - Model a decision or "what if" scenario without
> committing it.

### Signature interaction

**User:** "I spent \$100 at Dick's Sporting Goods for softball stuff for
my kid."

**Expected behavior:**

> **•** Match or create the transaction.
>
> **•** Understand the explicit amount, merchant, purpose, and person.
>
> **•** Connect it to the appropriate event and category.
>
> **•** Preserve the \$100 cash impact.
>
> **•** Remove it from misleading generic-shopping analysis.
>
> **•** Ask only if a material assumption remains, such as recurrence.
>
> **•** Recalculate affected insights.
>
> **•** Confirm exactly what changed and provide Undo.

### Confirmation pattern

| **"Got it. I matched the \$100 Dick's purchase and labeled it as your child's softball equipment under Softball Season. The cash impact is unchanged, but I will not treat it as ordinary shopping. Recurrence still needs confirmation. Change \| Undo"** |
|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|

### Interaction rules

> **•** Do not force a form when conversation provides enough
> information.
>
> **•** Do not make the user repeat details already in the transaction
> or profile.
>
> **•** Ask one focused follow-up instead of a multi-question
> interrogation.
>
> **•** Show the matched transaction or object before a material update.
>
> **•** Apply high-confidence, low-risk context changes with visible
> confirmation and Undo.
>
> **•** Require confirmation for ambiguous matches or changes that
> materially affect a recommendation.
>
> **•** Never say "done" unless the structured financial state actually
> changed.
>
> **•** Keep typed input available whenever microphone input is
> unavailable or undesirable.
>
> **•** Let users review and delete conversational history according to
> the retention policy.

### Accessibility and dignity

Voice is an option, not a requirement. It can reduce executive-function
burden, motor effort, spelling pressure, and category-selection fatigue,
but the product must remain fully usable by keyboard, screen reader, and
text input. The microphone should never start without an explicit user
action.

## Data pipeline

> **1.** Obtain informed consent.
>
> **2.** Receive financial data from Plaid or manual entry.
>
> **3.** Store source and timestamp for each data element.
>
> **4.** Normalize and deduplicate.
>
> **5.** Separate pending, posted, transfer, refund, income, debt
> payment, and outflow behavior.
>
> **6.** Calculate completeness and freshness.
>
> **7.** Suggest classifications and recurring patterns.
>
> **8.** Receive spoken or typed context when the user chooses to
> provide it.
>
> **9.** Transcribe voice input through an approved service when needed;
> do not retain raw audio by default.
>
> **10.** Identify intent and extract explicit entities such as amount,
> merchant, person, purpose, event, recurrence, and constraint.
>
> **11.** Resolve the utterance against candidate transactions or domain
> objects using deterministic matching and confidence thresholds.
>
> **12.** Ask for confirmation where a match or material meaning is
> ambiguous.
>
> **13.** Apply the authorized structured update through validated
> domain services, not through free-form model output.
>
> **14.** Recompute discoveries and scenarios through deterministic
> rules.
>
> **15.** Use AI to explain, summarize, or ask a focused clarifying
> question within policy.
>
> **16.** Store the engine version, inputs, context changes,
> confirmation or undo event, assumptions, and final decision.

>
> **•** Suggest categories or Financial Event links.
>
> **•** Summarize a financial picture.
>
> **•** Generate clarifying questions.

>
> **•** Suggest candidate transaction or Financial Event matches with
> confidence.
>
> **•** Compare precomputed scenarios.
>
> **•** Translate policy and engine output into the Covarify voice.
>
> **•** Help retrieve documents and prior decisions.

### AI may not

> **•** Invent missing balances, rates, dates, income, or obligations.
>
> **•** Infer sensitive life meaning without evidence and confirmation.
>
> **•** Override deterministic safeguards.
>
> **•** Present generated language as a verified creditor, tax, legal,
> or investment outcome.
>
> **•** Hide uncertainty.
>
> **•** Automatically execute high-consequence actions.
>
> **•** Directly mutate financial state without validated tools,

>
> **•** Pretend a transaction was matched when confidence is
> insufficient.
>
> **•** Retain raw microphone audio by default or outside the disclosed

>
> **•** Use personal financial data to train external models unless a

## Trust promise

Covarify handles information that can expose a person's vulnerabilities,

## Trust principles

> **•** Never sell personal financial information.

> **•** Do not rank recommendations based on undisclosed compensation.
>
> **•** Collect the minimum needed.
>
> **•** Explain why data is requested.

>

>
> **•** Do not use shame, fear, or false urgency.
>
> **•** Do not imply professional authority the company does not hold.
>
> **•** Preserve evidence and recommendation versions.

>

## Existing policy foundation

>
> **•** Privacy Policy.

>

These are governance assets, but they become real only when the

## Recommendation safety framework

### Safeguard 1 - Essential obligations

Do not recommend actions that knowingly jeopardize housing, necessary
utilities, food, essential transportation, urgent medical needs, or
critical insurance without clearly presenting the risk and alternative
support path.

### Safeguard 2 - Required payments

Distinguish a required minimum from an optional extra payment. "Pause
extra debt payments" must never be interpreted as "miss the required
payment."

### Safeguard 3 - Timing

Analyze the period through the next income event and upcoming
obligations. A monthly surplus can still produce a near-term shortage.

### Safeguard 4 - Consequence class

Low, moderate, and high-consequence actions require different levels of
explanation, confirmation, and professional handoff.

### Safeguard 5 - Data completeness

Do not present high confidence when missing accounts, rates, due dates,
or income could change the answer.

### Safeguard 6 - Incentive neutrality

Any referral, affiliate, lead, or partner compensation must be disclosed
and must not silently influence ranking.

### Safeguard 7 - No automatic execution

The MVP does not move money, apply for credit, close accounts, or enroll
users in financial products.

### Safeguard 8 - Conversational state changes

Do not let fluent language hide an uncertain match. Covarify must show
the transaction or object it interpreted, distinguish explicit facts
from inferred fields, ask when ambiguity is material, and provide
confirmation or Undo. A language model may propose a change; validated
product logic must execute it.

### PDR-024 - Every insight should naturally lead to an action

## New playbook decisions

### PDR-026 - First Win is an interactive decision, not a static tip

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Users can confirm transactions, protect constraints, compare actions,
and see the gap change before selecting a plan.

**Reason:** A static recommendation cannot reflect what is genuinely
flexible or acceptable in the user's life.

### PDR-027 - Recommendation order is Stabilize, Repair, Optimize, Build

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Covarify protects essentials and required payments before recommending
optimization or long-term allocation.

### PDR-028 - Consequence class controls product behavior

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Low, moderate, and high-consequence actions require increasing levels of
comparison, disclosure, confirmation, and professional handoff.

### PDR-029 - Required minimums and optional extra debt payments are distinct

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

The product must never blur a required payment with an optional
accelerated payoff amount.

### PDR-030 - Plaid is the primary input, not the product

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Connected data should lead when useful, while manual input remains a
valid and respected path.

### PDR-031 - No money movement in MVP

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Covarify helps users understand and decide. It does not initiate
transfers, payments, applications, or account closures in the MVP.

### PDR-032 - Every recommendation is explainable and versioned

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Store evidence, assumptions, engine version, user edits, action,
disclosure version, and review date.

### PDR-033 - Financial memory includes declined and protected choices

**Status:** PROPOSED FOR LOCK

Covarify should remember what the user marked as essential,
non-recurring, unacceptable, or preferred, with appropriate consent.

### PDR-034 - The conversational financial interface is a defining product capability

**Status:** LOCKED

Users can speak or type to Covarify in everyday language to ask
questions, explain transactions, add context, correct assumptions, state
constraints, and explore scenarios. Covarify converts the interaction
into verified answers or explicit, reviewable structured state changes
connected to the user's real financial data.

**Reason:** The meaning required for a good financial decision often
exists only in the user's explanation. Requiring the user to translate
that meaning into categories and forms recreates the work Covarify is
meant to remove.

**Signature example:** "I spent \$100 at Dick's Sporting Goods for
softball stuff for my kid." Covarify matches or creates the transaction,
records the explicit purpose and person, connects the Financial Event,
preserves the cash impact, updates affected analysis, asks only about
material ambiguity, and confirms the change with Undo.

**Guardrail:** A language model may interpret the statement, but
validated product services apply changes. Ambiguous matches require
clarification or confirmation. Conversation must never create an
invisible or unsupported mutation.

# Glossary

## Financial Clarity

The ability to understand the current picture, identify what matters
first, compare realistic options, and act with awareness of
consequences.

## Conversational Financial Interface

The spoken and typed interface through which a user asks questions,
explains meaning, corrects financial context, states constraints, and
explores decisions. It is connected to verified financial data and can
create explicit, reviewable structured updates.

## Conversation Turn

A spoken or typed user statement, question, correction, or instruction
with source, time, interpretation, and confirmation status.

## Context Assertion

A structured piece of user-provided or user-confirmed meaning, such as
person, purpose, category, event, recurrence, reimbursement, or
constraint.

## Conversational State Change

A validated update to a financial object or relationship produced from a
conversation turn, followed by recomputation, visible confirmation, and
an undo path where appropriate.

## Money Picture

The normalized view of accounts, cash, income, liabilities, obligations,
outflows, and timing.

## Financial Brain

The domain layer that interprets relationships among financial data and
life context.

## Financial Event

A meaning-level object connecting transactions, documents, people,
purpose, projects, and life context.

## Discovery

An evidence-backed observation that is relevant enough to deserve the
user's attention.

## Decision Engine

The deterministic and policy-controlled system that generates, models,
safeguards, and ranks action scenarios.

## First Win

The smallest evidence-backed decision or action that materially improves
stability, clarity, or momentum without hiding a foreseeable
consequence.

## Next Best Move

The recommended action after a First Win or at a later decision point,
based on updated data and context.

## Scenario

A modeled combination of actions and their estimated effects across a
defined period.

## Action Library

The structured catalog of possible user actions, including eligibility,
impact fields, consequence class, safeguards, and disclosures.

## Stabilize

Protect essentials, required payments, immediate liquidity, and critical
coverage.

## Repair

Close a recurring or near-term gap through realistic spending, timing,
payment, and income actions.

## Optimize

Improve cost, simplicity, debt structure, payment sequencing, or reserve
allocation after stability exists.

## Build

Use a durable positive margin for reserves, goals, protection, debt
reduction, or long-term wealth.

## Financial Zoom

The progressive-detail interaction model that begins with a calm summary
and lets users open evidence, assumptions, and scenario math.

## Financial Life Vault

The organized record of documents and context attached to Financial
Events, accounts, people, and decisions.

## Confidence

A product assessment of how strongly the available and confirmed
evidence supports a discovery or recommendation.

## Completeness

An assessment of whether the relevant accounts, dates, obligations, and
context are present.

## Constraint

A user-declared requirement or boundary, such as an expense that must
remain protected or an action the user will not take.

## Decision History

The preserved record of options, assumptions, user choices, rationale,
disclosures, review dates, and outcomes.

## Consequence Class

Low, moderate, or high classification that determines how a financial
action may be presented, confirmed, or escalated.

## Design Partner

An early user who agrees to structured observation, feedback, and
outcome follow-up to improve the product.

## Why Financial Memory is a product layer

Financial Memory is not generic AI memory and it is not simply a
transaction archive. It is the durable, user-visible continuity layer
that connects financial facts, life meaning, scenarios, decisions,
recommendations, outcomes, and evolving lessons. It exists so Covarify
can answer not only “What is true today?” but also “What did we decide,
why did we decide it, and what did we learn?”

## Canonical record types

| **Record**         | **Definition**                                                                                                                              | **Rule**                                                                                            |
|--------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Financial fact     | A sourced observation such as a transaction, balance, bill, income deposit, account state, user-entered value, confirmation, or correction. | Append only. Corrections create linked records and never silently rewrite history.                  |
| Scenario           | A preview of one or more possible choices and modeled consequences.                                                                         | Never changes the active plan until explicitly confirmed and applied.                               |
| Decision           | A confirmed choice with owner, source, scope, expected impact, assumptions, and reversal history.                                           | Versioned, traceable, and reversible where operationally possible.                                  |
| Recommendation     | A time-bound interpretation built from a known data snapshot.                                                                               | Preserve the original evidence and explanation even when superseded.                                |
| Memory observation | A user-visible pattern about what appears to work, fail, matter, or require protection.                                                     | Evidence-backed, confidence-scored, correctable, dismissible, and never treated as immutable truth. |
| Outcome            | What actually happened after a decision and whether the expected result occurred.                                                           | Used to improve future recommendations without rewriting the original decision.                     |

## Decision Ledger

The Decision Ledger is the audit-ready record of what Covarify heard,
what was previewed, what required confirmation, what became active, what
expired, and what was reversed. Publicly this may appear as Plan History
or Decisions.

- Active decisions - choices currently affecting the working plan.

- Pending confirmation - interpreted requests that have not been
  applied.

- Scenario history - options tested without commitment.

- Past decisions - completed, expired, superseded, dismissed, or
  reversed choices.

- Every record includes who or what initiated it, when, scope,
  rationale, expected impact, confidence, missing information, and Undo
  or reversal history.

## Multiple speakers and household safety

Voice input must not silently apply a material decision when speaker
identity is unclear. An unconfirmed spoken request becomes Pending
Confirmation. Household permissions, private views, delegated roles, and
approval rules are required before shared financial plans launch.

## Financial Memory principles

- Remember what mattered, not every conversational fragment.

- Store facts separately from interpretations.

- Explain why a recommendation changed instead of hiding the prior
  recommendation.

- Let users inspect, correct, dismiss, or retire what Covarify has
  learned.

- Record decisions, not raw audio, by default.

- Support export, correction, deletion, and legal retention obligations
  without undermining audit integrity.

- Use Financial Memory to strengthen user judgment, not create
  dependency.

## What every recommendation must explain

| **Question**             | **Required answer**                                                                                            |
|--------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| What do we know?         | The sourced facts, analysis window, accounts, and user-confirmed context.                                      |
| What are we assuming?    | Every classification, timing assumption, recurrence estimate, and modeled value.                               |
| What is missing?         | Material gaps such as APR, minimum due, due date, income reliability, household owner, or unconnected account. |
| Why this recommendation? | The chain from evidence to tradeoff to proposed action.                                                        |
| What could change it?    | New transactions, corrected context, updated debt terms, user priorities, or timing.                           |
| How confident are we?    | High, medium, or low with a plain-language reason.                                                             |

## Engineering implications

- Use append-only facts, immutable audit events, and versioned working
  plans.

- Use deterministic domain services for calculations and state mutation.

- Separate scenario, decision, recommendation, memory, and outcome
  schemas.

- Require authentication, encrypted persistence, least privilege, backup
  restoration, deletion workflows, and schema migration before real-user
  memory.

- Preserve engine version, prompt/model version where used, evidence
  IDs, assumptions, and disclosure version.

## UX implications

- Always show whether an item is Preview, Pending, Applied, Expired, or
  Reversed.

- Show what is currently affecting the plan and why.

- Allow users to ask “Why?”, “What changed?”, and “Have I tried this
  before?” anywhere in the experience.

- Make correction and Undo obvious.

- Present Financial Memory publicly as Your Financial Story or What We
  Have Learned Together.
